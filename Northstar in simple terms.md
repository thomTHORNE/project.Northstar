Northstar is your personal music library. It doesn't store audio files — it stores your relationship with music: the artists you love, the albums that matter, the playlists you've built, the moments you want to remember.

You bring your own audio sources — a streaming service, YouTube, cloud storage, or any combination. Northstar links to them and plays through them. What Northstar owns is everything else — your library, your structure, your context. If you switch services tomorrow, your library stays intact.

## What you can do with it

At its core, Northstar lets you build and manage a music library that is truly yours:

- **Import** your existing music from a streaming service, YouTube, or cloud storage — so you don't start from scratch
- **Organize** artists, albums, tracks, and playlists your way, independent of any streaming service's interface or algorithm
- **Tag** anything with your own labels — a mood, a memory, a context, whatever gives the music meaning to you
- **Write notes** on any artist, album, track, or playlist — why you love it, where you were when you found it, what it reminds you of
- **Capture** a listening session automatically — let Northstar record what you play so you never lose a moment worth keeping
- **Track your habits** — over time, Northstar learns your listening patterns and can surface connections you haven't noticed yourself

The idea is simple: music means something. Northstar gives you the space to capture that meaning, not just stream past it.

## Import

Most people already have music somewhere — years of saved albums on Spotify, liked songs on Apple Music, a YouTube playlist, audio files sitting in Google Drive. Import is how you bring all of that into Northstar without starting from scratch.

**From a streaming service** — connect your account and Northstar pulls in your existing library: artists, albums, tracks, and playlists, with all the relationships between them intact. It replicates the structure as a starting point. From there, it's yours to build on.

**From a link** — paste a URL to any track, album, or artist on a supported service, a YouTube video, or a file in your cloud storage. Northstar extracts what metadata it can and adds the entry to your library. This is the lightweight path — no full account connection needed, just a link to something you want in your collection.

Either way, once something is in Northstar, it belongs to your library. The source is just a reference — where Northstar goes to play the audio. The rest of the data is yours.

## Organize

Everything in Northstar is built around four building blocks: **artists**, **albums**, **tracks**, and **playlists**. These are the things your library is made of.

The relationship between them follows the natural shape of music: an artist makes albums, albums contain tracks. Playlists sit outside that hierarchy — they're your own groupings, cut across the library however you see fit.

What makes this different from a streaming service is ownership. When you add an artist or a track to Northstar, it lives in *your* library. You can rename things, restructure them, and arrange them however makes sense to you. Nothing gets rearranged by an update or hidden behind a new interface. It's yours.

Linking to a streaming service means Northstar knows where to find the actual audio. But the library — the structure, the relationships, the names — that belongs to you. If a service shuts down or you move to a different one, you update the link. The rest stays exactly as you left it.

There's no right or wrong way to organize. Some people build their library around artists. Others live in playlists. Northstar doesn't enforce a workflow — it gives you the structure and gets out of your way.

## Playlists

A playlist in Northstar can work in two ways.

The first is manual: you add tracks one by one, in the order you want, for whatever reason you want. A setlist, a road trip, a mood. You're in full control of what's in it and how it's arranged.

The second is tag-driven: instead of adding tracks directly, you define the playlist by a set of tags. Northstar then pulls in every track across your library that matches — automatically. You don't curate the contents; you define the conditions, and the playlist composes itself.

The tag-driven playlist stays alive. Tag a new track tomorrow and it appears in every playlist whose conditions it meets. Remove a tag and it's gone. The playlist always reflects the current state of your library.

These two modes can also be combined — a playlist with a fixed core of tracks you've chosen, plus a tag filter that brings in anything else that fits.

## Capture Mode

Capture Mode turns a playlist into a live recording of a listening session.

You enable it on any playlist, then just listen. Every track that plays gets added to that playlist automatically — in order, as it happened. No tapping "add to playlist", no interrupting the moment. When you're done, the playlist is a document of exactly what you heard and when.

This is useful in more ways than one. It's how you save a mood — a late-night session that had a particular energy you want to find your way back to. It's how you build a shortlist while browsing new music, letting your listening behavior do the curating instead of your memory. It's a record of a night with friends, preserved as a setlist.

Capture Mode runs quietly in the background. A small indicator lets you know it's active. When you're done, you turn it off. The playlist stays.

## Tag

Tags are labels you create and apply to anything in your library — an artist, an album, a track, or a playlist. There are no predefined categories. You decide what the tags are and what they mean.

A tag could be a mood: *melancholic*, *euphoric*, *focused*. A setting: *late night*, *morning run*, *long drive*. A period of your life: *summer 2019*, *breakup*, *moving to a new city*. Anything that gives a piece of music a place in your world.

Tags cut across the formal structure of your library. Where a manual playlist groups tracks you've deliberately chosen, a tag connects things that share something — regardless of artist, album, or playlist. It's a different kind of relationship, one built around meaning rather than structure.

Over time, your tags become a map of how you relate to music. And because they're yours — not generated by an algorithm — they mean exactly what you intended them to mean.

## Write notes

Every item in your library can hold a note — an artist, an album, a track, a playlist, even a tag. It's a free-form text field. No structure, no format required.

A note might be why you love a record, what you were going through when you found it, who introduced it to you, or simply something you noticed about the way it's made. It could be one sentence or several paragraphs. It's private, it's personal, and it stays attached to the thing it's about.

The intent behind this is straightforward: music is rarely just music. It carries context — people, places, periods of life. Notes are how you hold onto that context instead of letting it fade. A year from now you can open an album and remember not just that you loved it, but *why*.

This is also what separates Northstar from a music app that's purely about playback. Playback is consumption. Notes are reflection. The library becomes a record of your relationship with music over time, not just a list of things you've listened to.

## Track your habits

As you use Northstar, it quietly builds a picture of how you listen. What you play, when, how often, in what order. Not to sell you something — to give that data back to you.

Over time this becomes useful in ways that are hard to anticipate upfront. You might notice you gravitate toward certain artists on certain days. That a tag you created a year ago still dominates your most-played tracks. That there's a cluster of albums you return to every winter. Northstar surfaces these patterns without imposing conclusions — you decide what they mean.

It can also help with discovery. Because your library is yours — structured by you, tagged by you — recommendations drawn from it reflect your taste, not a generalized model of what people like you tend to enjoy. The input is specific, so the output is too.

This is the feature that grows with you. The longer you use Northstar, the more it knows about your relationship with music, and the more useful it becomes — not by replacing your judgment, but by informing it.

## Who it's for

**The Curator** collects intentionally. They have strong opinions about their library, hate algorithmic radio, and want full control over how everything is organized. They may use multiple streaming services and don't want to be locked into any one of them.

**The Sentimentalist** treats music as memory. A song isn't just a song — it's a place, a person, a feeling. They want to capture those moments and find their way back to them. Their library is a diary as much as it is a collection.

**The Sovereign Explorer** discovers music on their own terms. They're not interested in what's trending or what an algorithm thinks they'll like. They want their own listening history to guide them — patterns they've built themselves, not ones that have been built for them.
